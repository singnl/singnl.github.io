Audio Equalizer

3-band analog equalizer with passive RC filters and LM386 power amp

Overview

The objective of this final project was to design, construct, and evaluate an analog audio equalizer capable of separating an input audio signal into low, mid, and high-frequency bands, independently adjusting each frequency range, recombining the processed signals, and amplifying the final output to drive an 8Ω speaker load. The system was required to satisfy specified cutoff frequencies of approximately 320 Hz for the bass filter, 320 Hz to 3200 Hz for the mid-pass filter, and 3200 Hz for the treble filter, while also meeting voltage and output power requirements.

The designed equalizer utilized passive RC filters, potentiometer-based volume controls, an operational amplifier summing stage, and an LM386 power amplifier. Experimental frequency response analysis (FRA) curves were obtained for each filter stage to verify the -3 dB cutoff frequencies.

Results demonstrated that the system successfully met the design specifications within acceptable tolerances. Minor deviations from theoretical values were attributed primarily to component tolerances, breadboard parasitics, and measurement uncertainty.

Theory

The equalizer splits the input into three bands using passive RC filters: a low-pass for bass, a high-pass for treble, and a band-pass (low-pass and high-pass in series, joined by a buffer) for the mids. Both filter types share the same governing equation:

$$f_c = \frac{1}{2\pi RC}$$

Each band then runs through an inverting op-amp gain stage controlled by a potentiometer, letting bass/mid/treble levels be adjusted independently before an inverting summing amplifier recombines them and the LM386 boosts the result to drive the speaker:

$$P_{out} = \frac{V_{RMS}^2}{R_{load}}$$

Testing was done by sweeping a 1 Vpp signal from 10 Hz to 10 kHz on an oscilloscope, recording -3 dB cutoffs, and iterating on component values until every stage landed within the ±10% tolerance. A few resistor values changed along the way (e.g. the high-pass resistor moved from 47Ω to 56Ω, and the LM386 supply rail was bumped from 5V to 8V for headroom) to compensate for breadboard parasitics and component tolerances.

Results

Filter Cutoff Precision: All three filters landed within the ±10% tolerance — low-pass at 348.35 Hz (8.85% error), high-pass at 3.427 kHz (7.09% error), and band-pass spanning 345.3 Hz to 3.238 kHz.

Conclusions

The finished circuit successfully separates an input signal into three frequency bands, lets each be adjusted independently, and recombines and amplifies them into a usable audio output. All filter cutoffs, output voltages, and ripple met spec, and output power hit target at 1 kHz and 10 kHz with only a marginal 0.55% shortfall at 100 Hz. Most of the deviation from theoretical values traces back to standard component tolerances and breadboard parasitics rather than any design flaw — a good reminder that even a well-calculated circuit needs iterative tuning on real hardware.
